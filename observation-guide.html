What To Watch For: Spotting a Deep-Lying Playmaker In Your Child's Team
Five things to look for pitch-side that most parents (and a lot of coaches) miss.

Every team has a striker everyone watches. Hardly anyone watches the player who's actually dictating the game from deep. That player is often the most valuable one on the pitch, and the hardest to spot if you don't know what you're looking for.

Next time you're watching a match, try picking out one player and just watching for these five things. You'll start to see the game differently.

1
Scanning before the ball arrives
Watch their head, not their feet. A player with real game intelligence checks over their shoulder before the ball gets to them, not after. They already know what's around them before they have to deal with it.
CUE: Head turns in the 2-3 seconds before receiving
2
Playing on the half-turn
Most young players receive the ball facing the way it came from, then have to stop and turn. A deeper thinking player receives side-on, already facing forward, ready to play in one movement.
CUE: Body shape is angled, not square, when the ball arrives
3
Choosing forward over safe
Anyone can play sideways. Watch how often this player looks to play forward first, even under a bit of pressure, instead of automatically going backwards or square to keep possession.
CUE: First look is forward, not sideways
4
Finding the spare player quickly
A good deep player doesn't need three or four touches to work out who's free. They see it early and get the ball there fast, before the opposition can close the gap back down.
CUE: Decision and pass happen inside one or two touches
5
Getting on the ball, even when it's not going well
This is a big one. Weaker players hide when the team is under pressure. Players with real game intelligence ask for the ball more, not less, because they trust themselves to solve the problem.
CUE: Shows for the ball after a mistake, not just after a good moment
A note on how this actually gets built: none of the above comes from a coach shouting instructions from the sideline. It comes from a player being put in situations, over and over, where they have to work it out for themselves. If your child is always being told exactly where to pass, they're not being given the chance to develop this. The environment has to ask the question. The player has to find the answer.
